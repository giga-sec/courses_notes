2. Backtasking vs Switchtasking

(Productive) Backtask :
When a task doesn't need that much of an attention but is doing its work in the background
^EG: Bread baking in the background while you do pushups

(Uneffective) Switchtasking: Doing Two or More tasks that requires attention
^EG: Texting your lover (if you have one) and watching a youtube documentary
Talking on the phone while also handling email

Quote: "Biggest obstacle to effective time management isn't that we lack enough time to get things done. It's that we're trying to do too many things in the time we have available which is SLOW."

Example as to why Switchtasking decreases time:

(SCENARIO)
You're responding to an email from a coworker, you're crafting a careful response,
==(DISTRACTION) and then a text message comes in. ==
Most will stop typing the email and check the message. They send a quick reply, then they have to return to the email and do what? Reread what they wrote, get their mind in the right place, and continue writing. This costs a lot of extra time

Conclusion:
So, let's actually imagine focus as a moving meter.

  • If you focus on another task #2, your focus on that task #1 will decrease.
  • If you continue focusing on task #1, your focus on that task #1 will increase
    Note, I am referring to another task since by definition, switchtasking is focusing on two or more tasks that requires attention. If you let your mind wander, to take a break, you're not focusing on another task. You're simply letting your attention be in the background to that task #1
    Now, focus mean here is using your physical, mental and emotional you to complete that task.

MINDSETS - Principles of Productivity

Productivity Principle 1- Space

  1. Space
    A "gathering point".
    Meaning a place of unDone items (unread emails, lots of piled works on your desk)

Productivity Principle 2 - Mind

  1. Mind
    Unresolved tasks and projects create a lot of self-inflicted switchtasking. Don't use your mind as a gathering point.
    Comment: So perhaps use To Do List to jot down your tasks instead of relying your mind as a gathering point

Productivity Principle 3 - Time

  1. Time
    • (MINDSETS OF USING CALENDAR) Focused on what's most valuable

QUESTIONS from the Quiz

Q: How do you achieve maximum results during the limited amount of time you have at work each week?
A: - Focus most of your time on your most valuable activities (MVAs).

Q: What should you use your time budget calendar for?
A: planning your ideal day and week

Q: To find the best time for your recurring processing schedule, what should you consider?
A: The times at which you are at least likely to be interrupted

Q: A mind filled with tasks and to-dos is _____.
A: in a constant state of switching

Q: How should you use the list of mental triggers?
A: use the list to clear your mind from shits...

Q: After you've done some processing, you find that your task reminders list is growing very large. What might this mean?
A: You're probably not scheduling them enough of these tasks on your actual calendar

Q: What is the most efficient solution to keep personal and work emails separate, even if they are in a single email inbox?
A: Use different signatures and email accounts for personal and professional emails.

Q: You teach music lessons out of your home. You often have sheet music, handwritten notes on individual students, and financial records. What's a quick-but-effective strategy for your physical filing system?
A: Create a general alphabetical file for non-financial documents, a separate alphabetical file for student files, one for sheet music, plus a set of twelve-monthly folders for financial documents.
